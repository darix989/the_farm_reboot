#  Logical Fallacies: Sorted by Complexity

This list incorporates your specific terms into the cognitive complexity framework. This helps distinguish between simple emotional manipulation and deep structural failures in logic.

---

## Level 1: Surface-Level (Emotional & Social)
These fallacies are "shortcuts" that bypass logic entirely to trigger an immediate emotional or social response.

### 1. Ad Hominem
Attacking the person instead of the argument.
* **Example:** "You can't be right about taxes because you're a jerk."

### 2. Appeal to Popularity (Bandwagon)
Assuming something is true because many people believe it.
* **Example:** "Everyone is buying this stock, so it's a guaranteed win."

### 3. Straw Man
Distorting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack.
* **Example:** "You want to lower the military budget? I can't believe you want our country to be defenseless."

### 4. Appeal to Pity (Ad Misericordiam)
Using feelings of pity or guilt to win an argument rather than facts.
* **Example:** "I know I failed the exam, but my car broke down and my dog is sick; please give me a passing grade."

### 5. Appeal to Fear (Ad Baculum)
Using threats or scaring the audience into agreement.
* **Example:** "If we don't pass this law immediately, our streets will be overrun with chaos by next week."

### 6. Appeal to Emotion
A broad category (including pity and fear) where any emotion—hope, anger, pride—is used to substitute for evidence.
* **Example:** "Imagine the look of joy on a child's face; that's why you should support this expensive bill."

### 7. Glittering Generalities
Using vague, "virtue-word" slogans that sound great but have no concrete meaning.
* **Example:** "Vote for me for a future of pure Freedom, Hope, and Prosperity."

---

## Level 2: Intermediate (Faulty Logic & Connections)
These require you to analyze the *link* between the premise and the conclusion. The error is in how the argument is built.

### 8. False Dilemma (Either/Or)
Presenting two choices as the only options when more exist.
* **Example:** "Either you're with us, or you're with the terrorists."

### 9. Slippery Slope
Claiming one small step will lead to a chain of disasters without proof of the connection.
* **Example:** "If we let people work from home, soon no one will ever leave their houses and the retail economy will vanish."

### 10. Hasty Generalization
Making a broad claim based on a tiny or non-representative sample.
* **Example:** "I saw a teenager littering today. This whole generation has no respect for the environment."

### 11. Weak Analogy
Comparing two things that aren't actually alike in the way being argued.
* **Example:** "Clogged arteries require surgery to save a life, so a 'clogged' government requires a total revolution."

### 12. Loaded Question
Asking a question that has a controversial assumption built into it, so it can't be answered without sounding guilty.
* **Example:** "Have you stopped cheating on your taxes yet?" (Answering 'yes' or 'no' both admit to cheating).

---

## Level 3: Advanced (Structural & Conceptual)
These are the hardest to detect because they involve manipulating the internal rules of the debate or the definitions of words.

### 13. Special Pleading
Applying a universal rule to everyone else while claiming you are an exception without a valid reason.
* **Example:** "Everyone should wait their turn in line, but I'm in a really big hurry for a meeting, so I should go first."

### 14. "Nothing to Hide" Fallacy
The claim that surveillance is fine because "if you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear."
* **Logic Failure:** It assumes privacy is only for those doing wrong, ignoring the fundamental right to personal space and the potential for the abuse of power.

### 15. Special Pleading / No True Scotsman
Shifting the goalposts or redefining terms to protect a claim from a valid counter-argument.
* **Example:** "No real programmer uses that language."
